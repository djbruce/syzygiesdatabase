--- layout: default title: Approach description: SyzygyData, a project about computing syzygies. ---

We approach the problem of computing syzygies of \(\mathbb{P}^n\) via computing the cohomology of the Koszul complex. Continuing the notation from Background page \(K_{p,q}(n,b;d)\) may be computed as the cohomology of the complex: \[\bigwedge^{p+1}S_{d}\otimes S_{b+(q-1)d}\xrightarrow{\quad \partial_{p+1} \quad}\bigwedge^{p}S_{d}\otimes S_{b+qd}\rightarrow{\quad \partial_{p} \quad}\bigwedge^{p-1}S_{d}\otimes S_{b+(q+1)d},\] where the differentials are given by \[\partial_{p}(m_1\wedge m_2\wedge \cdots \wedge m_p\otimes f)=\sum_{k=1}^p(-1)^km_1\wedge m_2\wedge \cdots \wedge \hat{m}_{k}\wedge \cdots \wedge m_p\otimes(m_kf).\] Note these differentials respect the \(\mathbb{Z}^{n+1}\)-grading on \(S\), and so the above complex may be decomposed into its multigraded stands, and so we may compute \(K_{p,q}(n,b;d)_{\textbf{a}}\) via the cohomology of the following subcomplex: \[\left(\bigwedge^{p+1}S_{d}\otimes S_{b+(q-1)d}\right)_{\textbf{a}}\xrightarrow{\quad \partial_{p+1,\textbf{a}} \quad}\left(\bigwedge^{p}S_{d}\otimes S_{b+qd}\right)_{\textbf{a}}\rightarrow{\quad \partial_{p,\textbf{a}} \quad}\left(\bigwedge^{p-1}S_{d}\otimes S_{b+(q+1)d}\right)_{\textbf{a}}.\] Thus, computing the multigraded Betti numbers \(\beta_{p,\textbf{a}}(n,b;d)\) reduces to the linear algebra problem of computing the ranks of \(\partial_{p+1,\textbf{a}}\) and \(\partial_{p+1,\textbf{a}}\). Rmk We denote the \(k\)th graded piece of \(S\) by \(S_k\). Now for any \(b\in \mathbb{Z}\) and \(d\in\mathbb{N}\) we let \[ S(b;d):= \bigoplus_{k\in \mathbb{Z}}S_{dk+b}\subset S\] which we think of as an \(R=\text{Sym}(S_d)\)-module. Notice that \(S(b;d)\) corresponds to the pushforward of the sheaf \(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(b)\) by the \(d\)'uple embedding of \(\mathbb{P}^n\).

Understanding the Betti numbers of \(S(b;d)\) amounts, in essence, to understanding \(\text{Tor}_{p}^{R}(S(b;d),\mathbb{C})\) as a graded \(R\)-module. Usually by "graded" one means the \(\mathbb{Z}\) these \(\text{Tor}\) modules inherit from the standard grading on \(S\). However, in our work there turns out to be three different, although related, gradings we find it fruitful to consider. We briefly describe each below:

Standard Decomposition

As mentioned above \(\text{Tor}^R(S(b;d),\mathbb{C})\) inherits a natural \(\mathbb{Z}\)-grading from the standard grading on \(S\). This gives rise to thestandard graded Betti number of \(S(b;d)\) which one may define by: \[\beta_{p,q}(S(b;d)):=\dim_{\mathbb{C}}\text{Tor}_{p}^{R}(S(b;d),\mathbb{C})_{q}.\] Note while this notation is quite standard, for easy of notation as well as other applications it is often easier to work with the Koszul cohomology groups of \(S(b;d)\), which are defined to be: \[K_{p,q}(S(b;d))=\text{Tor}_{p}^{R}(S(b;d),\mathbb{C})_{p+q}.\] Notice knowing these Koszul cohomology groups is equivalent to know the standard graded Betti numbers since \(\dim K_{p,q}(n,b;d)\) is equal to \(\beta_{p,p+q}(n,b;d)\). The (graded) Betti table of \(S(b;d)\) is the matrix who's \((p,q)\) entry is \(\dim K_{p,q}(n,b;d)\). Rmk

Schur Decomposition

The polynomial ring \(S\) carries an action of the general linear group \(\textbf{GL}_{n+1}(\mathbb{C})\), which in turns gives \(S\) a \(\mathbb{Z}^3\)-grading. Rmk We call this the Schur grading and it descends to \(\text{Tor}^{R}(S(b;d),\mathbb{C})\). Moreover, since this action is degree preserving this grading further descends to the Koszul cohomology groups. We call the resulting decomposition the Schur decomposition of \(K_{p,q}(n,b;d\). Since the irreducible representations of \(\textbf{GL}_{n+1}(\mathbb{C})\) are given (up to isomorphisms) Schur functors, we can write the Schur decomposition of \(K_{p,q}(n,b;d)\) can be expressed as: \[K_{p,q}(n,b;d)=\bigoplus_{\lambda\in\mathbb{Z}^3}\textbf{S}_{\lambda}(\mathbb{C}^{n+1})^{\oplus \beta_{p,\lambda}(n,b;d)},\] where \(\textbf{S}_{\lambda}\) is a Schur functor and \(\lambda\) has weight \(d(p+q)+b\). Rmk We call the \(\beta_{p,\lambda}(n,b;d)\) the Schur graded Betti numbers of \(S(b;d)\).

Multigraded Decomposition

The final grading we will make use of is the \(\mathbb{Z}^{n+1}\)-multigrading on \(S\) that arrises either by letting \(\text{deg}(x_i)=\textbf{e}_i\) where \(\textbf{e}_0,\ldots,\textbf{e}_n\)\) is the standard basis or by specializing the \(\textbf{GL}_{n+1}(\mathbb{C})\) action to an action of \((\mathbb{C}^*)^{n+1}\). From this second description we immediately see that this act also descends to the Koszul cohomology groups: \[K_{p,q}(n,b;d)=\bigoplus_{\textbf{a}\in\mathbb{Z}^{n+1}}\textbf{k}(\textbf{a})^{\oplus\beta_{p,\textbf{a}}(b;d)},\] where \(\textbf{k}(\textbf{a})\) is the \((\mathbb{C}^*)^{n+1}\)-representation given by \((z_0,\cdots,z_{n+1})\cdot w=(z_0^{a_0}\cdots z_{n+1}^{a_{n+1}})w.\) We call this the muligraded decomposition of \(K_{p,q}(n,b;d)\) and call the \(\beta_{p,\textbf{a}}(n,d;d)\) the multigraded Betti numbers. Rmk

Note that the multigraded Betti numbers determine both the standard and Schur graded Betti numbers. For example, we can recover the standard graded Betti numbers from the multigraded via the following formula: \[ \beta_{p,q}(n,b;d)=\sum_{|\textbf{a}|=d(p+q)+b}\beta_{p,\textbf{a}}(n,b;d). \] Recovering the Schur graded Betti numbers is slightly more complicated, but an algorithm for doing so can be found in the accompanying paper. Hence the largest part of our computations are focused on computing the multigraded Betti numbers. See Approach page for a discussion of how we go about this.

Note essentially all of what follows holds over any algebraically closed field of characteristic. Our choice of working ovr \(\mathbb{C}\) is simply one of connivence.

For notational hygiene we also often write \(K_{p,q}(n,b;d)\) for \(K_{p,q}(S(b;d))\), and likewise write \(\beta_{p,q}(n,b;d)\) for \(\beta_{p,q}(S(b;d))\).

Recall that we can think of a grading of a ring/module as a decomposition into irreducible representations arising from some group action. For example, the standard grading on \(S\) can be thought of coming from the natural \(\textbf{k}^*\).

Recall the weight of an element \(\lambda\in \mathbb{Z}^{n+1}\) is defined to be \(|\lambda|=\lambda_0+\lambda_1+\cdots+ \lambda_n\).

Notice that if \(\beta_{p,\textbf{a}}(n,b;d)\neq0\) then \(\textbf{a}\) must have weight \(d(p+q)+b\).
