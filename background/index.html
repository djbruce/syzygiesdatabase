Fix \(n>0\) and let \(S=\textbf{k}[x_0,\ldots,x_n]\) be the standard graded polynomial ring. We denote the \(k\)th graded piece of \(S\) by \(S_k\). Now for any \(b\in \mathbb{Z}\) and \(d\in\mathbb{N}\) we let \[ S(b;d):= \bigoplus_{k\in \mathbb{Z}}S_{dk+b}\subset S\] which we think of as an \(R=\text{Sym}(S_d)\)-module. Notice that \(S(b;d)\) corresponds to the pushforward of the sheaf \(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(b)\) by the \(d\)'uple embedding of \(\mathbb{P}^n\).

Understanding the Betti numbers of \(S(b;d)\) amounts, in essence, to understanding \(\text{Tor}_{p}^{R}(S(b;d),\textbf{k})\) as a graded \(R\)-module. Usually by "graded" one means the \(\mathbb{Z}\) these \(\text{Tor}\) modules inherit from the standard grading on \(S\). However, in our work there turns out to be three different, although related, gradings we find it fruitful to consider. We briefly describe each below:

Standard Decomposition

As mentioned above \(\text{Tor}^R(S(b;d),\textbf{k})\) inherits a natural \(\mathbb{Z}\)-grading from the standard grading on \(S\). This gives rise to thestandard graded Betti number of \(S(b;d)\) which one may define by: \[\beta_{p,q}(S(b;d)):=\dim_{\bf{k}}\text{Tor}_{p}^{R}(S(b;d),\textbf{k})_{q}.\] Note while this notation is quite standard, for easy of notation as well as other applications it is often easier to work with the Koszul cohomology groups of \(S(b;d)\), which are defined to be: \[K_{p,q}(S(b;d))=\text{Tor}_{p}^{R}(S(b;d),\textbf{k})_{p+q}.\] Notice knowing these Koszul cohomology groups is equivalent to know the standard graded Betti numbers since \(\dim K_{p,q}(S(b;d))\) is equal to \(\beta_{p,p+q}(S(b;d))\). The (graded) Betti table of \(S(b;d)\) is the matrix who's \((p,q)\) entry is \(\dim K_{p,q}(S(b;d))\).

Schur Decomposition

The polynomial ring \(S\) carries an action of the general linear group \(\textbf{GL}_{n+1}(\textbf{k})\), which in turns gives \(S\) a \(\mathbb{Z}^3\)-grading. (Remark) This grading descends to \(\Tor^{R}(S(b;d),\textbf{k})\). Moreover, since this action is degree preserving this grading further descends to the Koszul cohomology groups. We call the resulting decomposition, which may be written as: \[K_{p,q}(S(b;d))=\bigoplus_{\lambda\in\mathbb{Z}^3}\textbf{S}_{\lambda}(\textbf{k}^{n+1})^{\oplus m_{\lambda}}\] where \(\textbf{S}_{\lambda}\) is a Schur functor, the Koszul decomposition of \(K_{p,q}(S(b;d)\).

Multigraded Decomposition

As mentioned above \(\text{Tor}^R(S(b;d),\textbf{k})\) inherits a natural \(\mathbb{Z}\)-grading from the standard grading on \(S\). This gives rise to thestandard graded Betti number of \(S(b;d)\) which one may define by: \[\beta_{p,q}(S(b;d)):=\dim_{\bf{k}}\text{Tor}_{p}^{R}(S(b;d),\textbf{k})_{q}.\] Note while this notation is quite standard, for easy of notation as well as other applications it is often easier to work with the Koszul cohomology groups of \(S(b;d)\), which are defined to be: \[K_{p,q}(S(b;d))=\text{Tor}_{p}^{R}(S(b;d),\textbf{k})_{p+q}.\] Notice knowing these Koszul cohomology groups is equivalent to know the standard graded Betti numbers since \(\dim K_{p,q}(S(b;d))\) is equal to \(\beta_{p,p+q}(S(b;d))\). The (graded) Betti table of \(S(b;d)\) is the matrix who's \((p,q)\) entry is \(\dim K_{p,q}(S(b;d))\).

Recall that we can think of a grading of a ring/module as a decomposition into irreducible representations arising from some group action. For example, the standard grading on \(S\) can be thought of coming from the natural \(\textbf{k}^\times\).
